Research

Anatomical mapping of leaf water content. Poster presentation at the 2025 Society for Integrative and Comparative Biology Annual Meeting in Atlanta, Georgia, USA, on 3-7 January 2025.

Leaf water storage has a significant impact on whole plant susceptibility to functional decline under drought, however, water storage structures have an inherent carbon cost. While several indices exist to quantify stored water in ecological and physiological studies, these indices do not account for the functional implications of water stored in different tissues. This study seeks to improve our understanding of leaf water storage anatomy and its implications for physiological processes. We presented a novel experiment designed to quantitively test outstanding gaps in the literature focusing on three objectives: (1) use tissue-specific anatomical and compositional traits to predict leaf water mass per area (LWMA), (2) determine the relative contributions of each tissue to LWMA across diverse species, and (3) understand the implications of variance in leaf water storage anatomy for drought tolerance. Towards these objectives, we developed a theoretical model; Exhaustive Cross-Sectional Anatomy and Composition of Tissue Water model (EXACTw). When parameterized with empirical data for 17 diverse species, we found high precision in prediction of LWMA from anatomical traits when compared to empirical observations (R2 = 0.83, P<0.001, slope = 0.63). We concluded that although the bulk of water leaf water occurs in the photosynthetic tissues, variance in water storage across non-photosynthetic tissue (i.e., bundle sheath extension, veins, and epidermis) had a stronger impact on leaf drought tolerance indices extracted from leaf pressure-volume curves.

Anatomical mapping of apoplastic and symplastic leaf water content. Poster presentation at the University of Florida, Plant Science Club Symposium (PSC) annual meeting. Gainesville, Florida. 22-23, January,2024.

Leaf water storage has a significant impact on whole plant susceptibility to functional decline under drought; however, structures to maintain pools of usable water have an inherent carbon investment cost and reduce leaf volume used for metabolic processes. Fundamentally, water can be stored in the continuous living cytoplasm within cells (symplastic), or within the cell wall matrix, (apoplastic). While bulk leaf saturated water content (SWC) has been defined across species, the economic or physiological implications of its spatial distribution are less well understood. Therefore, our objectives are to (1) Anatomically predict SWC from anatomical tissue composition of the leaf, (2.) determine the relative contributions of each tissue to swc across diverse species, and (3.) understand the implications of variance in leaf water storage anatomy for drought tolerance. We applied anatomical measurements from 7 diverse species to a novel model to estimate saturated water content.

Mesomorphy and vulnerability indices of Solanum melongena and Corchorus olitorius in Nsukka metropolis. Thesis presentation at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka, Plant Science and Biotechnology department. Nsukka, Enugu, Nigeria. August 2019.

This study evaluated the vulnerability and mesomorphic indices of two woody farm shrubs (Solanum melongena and Corchorus olitorius) from three locations (Odoru, Ogige, Odenigwe) using their anatomical structures. The sectioning of the plant stems was carried out in the anatomy laboratory of the department of Plant science and Biotechnology, University of Nigeria Nsukka

About

I am Ogonna, a PhD student in Informatics and Computing with a multidisciplinary background that spans geospatial analysis, remote sensing, and ecological informatics. My research sits at the intersection of environmental systems and data-driven technologies, where I explore how spatial data, climate models, and machine learning can unravel complex patterns in forest mortality and species distributions.

Originally trained in the rigorous methodologies of spatial statistics and environmental modeling, I have since broadened my toolkit to include high-performance computing, satellite image processing, and field-based ecological validation. Whether it is mapping the subtle spread of beetle infestations across decades or building robust time-series models to predict environmental change, I thrive on projects that blend computation, critical inquiry, and real-world impact.

Beyond research, I am deeply interested in how data narratives shape policy, community resilience, and our collective understanding of landscape change. My goal is to produce work that is both scientifically rigorous and directly relevant to on-the-ground decision-making. When Iam not writing code or wrangling raster data, you will find me mentoring students, engaging in science communication, or collaborating across disciplines to tackle big environmental questions.
