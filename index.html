Research

Anatomical mapping of apoplastic and symplastic leaf water content. Poster presentation at the University of Florida, Plant Science Club Symposium (PSC) annual meeting. Gainesville, Florida. 22-23, January,2024.

Leaf water storage has a significant impact on whole plant susceptibility to functional decline under drought; however, structures to maintain pools of usable water have an inherent carbon investment cost and reduce leaf volume used for metabolic processes. Fundamentally, water can be stored in the continuous living cytoplasm within cells (symplastic), or within the cell wall matrix, (apoplastic). While bulk leaf saturated water content (SWC) has been defined across species, the economic or physiological implications of its spatial distribution are less well understood. Therefore, our objectives are to (1) Anatomically predict SWC from anatomical tissue composition of the leaf, (2.) determine the relative contributions of each tissue to swc across diverse species, and (3.) understand the implications of variance in leaf water storage anatomy for drought tolerance. We applied anatomical measurements from 7 diverse species to a novel model to estimate saturated water content.

Mesomorphy and vulnerability indices of Solanum melongena and Corchorus olitorius in Nsukka metropolis. Thesis presentation at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka, Plant Science and Biotechnology department. Nsukka, Enugu, Nigeria. August 2019.

This study evaluated the vulnerability and mesomorphic indices of two woody farm shrubs (Solanum melongena and Corchorus olitorius) from three locations (Odoru, Ogige, Odenigwe) using their anatomical structures. The sectioning of the plant stems was carried out in the anatomy laboratory of the department of Plant science and Biotechnology, University of Nigeria Nsukka

About

Nigeria is still practicing rain dependent arboriculture which as a result renders her vulnerable to the adverse effects of climate change. Extreme climatic events such as flooding, extreme heat, and drought has led to soil degradation which results in low crop yields, trees damage, and death. Understanding the response of plants to increase drought would be desirable in the light of global and regional changes, not only to forecast population dynamics in natural ecosystem, but also to adjust management practices; this inspired me to study Plant sciences with research focus in functional anatomy and ecophysiology, this work has exposed me to some lab works, field works, data science and Visualization using R, hyperspectral imaging and geospatial analysis. My research will be to study the various habitats where different species can grow optimally and understanding the impact of environment and anatomy on the physiology of trees and plants.

My current research for my Msc degree seeks to use functional traits (e.g., leaf saturated water content), anatomy (mathematical model using dimension of plant cells and tissues) and physiology (leaf water potential, turgor loss point) to understand the mechanisms linking leaf water storage to drought resilience. This work conceptually builds on my undergraduate thesis work in which I investigated local adaptation of Solanum melogena and Corchorus olitorus in the Nsukka Metropolis of Nigeria.

Committed to fostering diversity and inclusion in STEM, I have been working towards breaking this bias against careers in STEM for Nigerian women. I have been encouraging women by acting as a speaker and invited guest, acting as a mentor to minority women interested in a career in STEM (Undergraduate student in Nigeria & University of Florida), providing resources to encourage more women from Africa and other minority group to pursue a career in STEM. I address the challenges I face as a minority and ways I overcome them.
